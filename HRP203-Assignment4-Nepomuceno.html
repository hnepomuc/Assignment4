Introduction

Smoking is the leading preventable cause of cardiovascular disease and leads to 1 in every 4 U.S. deaths from cardiovascular disease. Inflammatory mediators caused by smoking lead to atherosclerosis and downstream elevated risk of coronary artery disease, stroke, peripheral artery disease, and elevated risk of lung and non-pulmonary cancers. This study sought to determine the association between smoking, cardiovascular risk, and health care cost in a simulated patient cohort. The overarching objective of this report is to demonstrate reproducible workflows for a course on methods for reproducible health research.

Methods

The data source was a simulated data set “cohort.csv” (n = 5000). The dataset contains individual-level information on smoking status, sex, age, cardiac events, and health care costs. Smoking status, sex, and incidence of cardiac events were recorded as binary variables. Age was defined as a continuous variable. Health care cost was defined as a continuous variable in U.S. dollars ($).

The primary outcome of the study was the association between smoking status and cardiac events. The secondary outcome of the study was the association between cardiac events and health care costs.

A logistic regression model was used to estimate the association between smoking and cardiac event incidence.

\[\text{logit}\bigl(\Pr(\text{cardiac}_i = 1) )= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \,\text{age}_i + \beta_2 \,\text{female}_i + \beta_3 \,\text{smoke}_i\]

Linear regression was used to estimate the association between cardiac events and health care costs.

\[E[cost] = β₀ + β₁(smoke) + β₂(female) + β₃(age)\] Both models adjusted for age and sex. A p value of <0.05 was considered significant.

Reproducibility statement: All results were produced in R version 4.5.3 (2026-03-11) and rendered with Quarto. The simulated data, analytic code, and this rendered document are tracked together in a public GitHub repository. The report may be reproduced end-to-end by cloning the repository and rendering the file. The repository for this report can be found at: https://github.com/hnepomuc/Assignment4.

Adjusted linear regression analysis to assess associations with health care costs was performed with results displayed in Table 3. Findings demonstrated that each cardiac event was associated with $519.97 higher health care costs (p<0.001). Each additional year of age was associated with $16.76 higher health care costs (p<0.001). Smoking status resulted in $510.87 higher hospital costs (p<0.001). Female sex was again protective, associated with an average health care savings of $271.15.

Discussion

In this simulated cohort, there was a significant positive association between smoking and our primary and secondary outcomes. Smoking status was associated with a higher incidence of cardiac events and higher health care costs, and this difference persisted after adjustment for age, sex, and smoking status. Additional findings included that female sex was associated with lower cost, and there was a statistically significant increase in cost for every 1-year increase in age.

These results are limited by the simulated data set, which limits its external validity. This analysis is intended as an exercise in reproducible statistical reporting and is not intended to provide real-world evidence.
